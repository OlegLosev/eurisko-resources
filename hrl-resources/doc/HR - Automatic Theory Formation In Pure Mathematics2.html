Here's the latest of HR's integer sequences added to the Online Encyclopedia of Integer Sequences:

[A047983]: 0,0,1,0,2,0,3,1,1,2,4,0,5,3,4,0,6,1,7,2,5,6,8,0,2,7,8,3,9,1,10,4,9,10,...

This is the number of integers less than n but with the same number of divisors as n. HR found this sequence using its new "common" production rule.

HR has been reported in the spring 1999 issue of "New Electronics", in an article on machine creativity by David Boothroyd:

...But two years ago, a program written at the Argonne National Laboratory in Illinois achieved a mathematical proof that had beaten some of the finest mathematicians for decades. Called the Robbins Conjecture, it was suggested by Herbert Robbins in the 1930s. It relates to a set of three equations and the task was to prove that they were equivalent to a Boolean Algebra. It has been hailed as one of the true breakthroughs in automated theorem proving.

"It was a major event in automated mathematics," says Simon Colton, a mathematical researcher at Edinburgh University's School of Artificial Intelligence. "This was a 65 year old conjecture that was finally proved by a computer theorem prover."

Colton himself works in this area, having developed a program called HR that invents definitions in pure mathematics. For example, the following sequence was produced when HR was looking at number theory: 1, 2, 8, 9, 12, 18, 24, 36, 40, 56, 60, 72, 80, 84, 88, 96 ,... These are `refactorable numbers', where the number of divisors is itself a divisor. An example is 9, which has 3 divisors, (1,3 and 9), and 3 divides 9.

"It appears that such numbers have been overlooked by mathematicians and we've proved some nice results about them", Colton says. "For example, all odd refactorable numbers are square numbers. HR now not only invents definitions, but also makes conjectures about them and either proves the conjecture, or finds a counterexample. It is particularly effective in algebraic domains, such as group theory and ring theory."

HR works in pure mathematics, so practical applications are hardly likely to be immediate, but then that is true of human work in this field.

Another significant mathematical program is Graffiti, which produces conjectures in what is known as graph theory. Some of these have been proved or disproved by some of the world's greatest mathematicians, such as the late Paul Erdos.

It is perhaps not surprising that creative computing would appear first in mathematics. Mathematics is, in many ways, a self contained world where the expertise required is highly specific and constrained in nature....

A New Scientist article has discussed HR (5th September 1998, page 17):

... The Brighton conference also heard that the University of Edinburgh has used a similar program, called HR, to make mathematical findings. Last month, HR "discovered" a new sequence of integers, called refactorables. The sequence has been accepted into the Encyclopedia of Online Sequences, the main global repository for such information, the researchers say.

Toby Walsh, a member of the Edinburgh team, says HR's intelligence comprises a set of heuristics, or human rules of thumb. "The heuristics are sufficiently strong that we don't need to do too much brute-force searching", he says. Both Muggleton and Walsh are quick to point out that scientists should not hang up their lab coats. "What we're doing will help mathematicians understand how they do mathematics, so they may be able to do it better", says Walsh ...
