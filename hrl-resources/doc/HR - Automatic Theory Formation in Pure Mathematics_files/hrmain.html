1. INTRODUCTION

HR is a system designed to invent and assess mathematical definitions. Imagine sitting down with a pen and paper and writing down the factorisation of the integers:

1 = 1 x 1
2 = 1 x 2 = 2 x 1
3 = 1 x 3 = 3 x 1
4 = 1 x 4 = 2 x 2 = 4 x 1
etc.,

Then, after trying out some ideas and making some calculations, imagine selecting these numbers for special attention:

1, 4, 9, 16, 25, ...

In this case, you've re-invented the concept of square numbers. You could equally have singled out the prime numbers:

2, 3, 5, 7, 11, 13, 17, ...

This is what HR does. Further, HR provides reasons why certain concepts are interesting, either by showing how novel the concept is, or how good it is at classification, or by proving theorems about it. HR then uses the most interesting concepts to build new concepts from, and creates a theory which includes:

  • Definitions of concepts
  • Calculations
  • Models (eg. groups)
  • Theorems and proofs
  • Open conjectures

This theory is then written up using the standard mathematical typesetting language, LaTeX, and presented to the user. HR also provides other ways in which the user can investigate HR's findings, such as a set of graphs showing how each concept was produced.

HR has been developed mainly in the domain of group theory. However, HR can work in many different domains of pure mathematics, including any finite algebra (quasigroup theory, ring theory, loop theory, etc), number theory and graph theory.

This note gives an overview of how HR builds and assesses concepts, how it makes and assesses conjectures about the concepts and how it uses third party theorem prover Otter and model generator MACE to settle the conjectures. After some indication of how HR forms theories in pure mathematics, and a brief guide to using the program, an assessment of HR in group theory, number theory and graph theory is given. Finally, I look at some possible ways in which HR could be developed in the future, provide some links to papers which contain more detail about HR, and provide some links to machine discovery in science.

This web-page is designed both to provide an informal overview of what HR does, and to provide a repository where I can store interesting results, statistics, facts, etc. Therefore, while the information here is fairly non-technical, there is a lot of it!
